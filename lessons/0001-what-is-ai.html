What Is AI — and Where Did It Come From?

The big picture before the details. By the end, you'll be able to explain AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning in plain English.

1. What Is Intelligence?

Before we can define Artificial Intelligence, we need to agree on what intelligence means. This turns out to be surprisingly tricky — even scientists debate it.

A working definition that most people accept:

Definition
Intelligence is the ability to acquire knowledge, reason with it, and use it to adapt to new situations in order to achieve goals.

Think about what you do when you learn a new game. You pick up the rules (acquire knowledge), figure out strategies (reason), and adjust when things don't go as planned (adapt). That's intelligence in action.

Analogy
A chess player is intelligent not because they memorize every possible game (impossible — there are more chess games than atoms in the universe), but because they learn patterns and apply them to new positions they've never seen before.

2. What Is Artificial Intelligence?

Now that we have intelligence defined, Artificial Intelligence (AI) is simply:

Definition
AI is the simulation of human intelligence by machines — especially computers — so that they can perform tasks that normally require human intelligence.

These tasks include things like: recognizing your face in a photo, understanding spoken words, translating text, playing games, or diagnosing diseases from medical scans.

The core idea is this: instead of programming a machine with a list of explicit rules, we try to give it the ability to learn.

3. The Relationship: AI → Machine Learning → Deep Learning

This is one of the most commonly tested concepts. Think of three nested circles:

AI ⊃ Machine Learning ⊃ Deep Learning

🧠 Artificial Intelligence (AI)
The broad field of making machines smart. Any technique that lets a computer do something that seems intelligent. The biggest circle.
📊 Machine Learning (ML)
A subset of AI. Instead of writing explicit rules, we give the machine data and let it find the rules itself. The machine "learns" from examples.
🔮 Deep Learning (DL)
A subset of ML. Uses structures called neural networks (inspired loosely by the brain) that have many layers. This is what powers image recognition, ChatGPT, etc.
⚠ Exam Alert
Remember the containment order: AI ⊃ ML ⊃ DL. All Deep Learning is Machine Learning, and all Machine Learning is AI — but not the other way around. Exam questions often test this direction.

4. The Three Schools of AI Thought

Historically, researchers have disagreed on how to make machines intelligent. Three major "schools" emerged:

🔣 Symbolism (Symbolic AI)
Believes intelligence is about symbols and logic. Write down rules, facts, and logical relationships. Let the machine reason with them. Think: expert systems that encode a doctor's rules as "IF symptom X AND symptom Y THEN disease Z."
Strength: Explainable. Weakness: Can't handle ambiguity or things you forgot to write rules for.
🕸 Connectionism
Believes intelligence comes from connections between many simple units (like neurons). This is the basis of neural networks and deep learning — the dominant approach today.
Strength: Learns from raw data without hand-crafted rules. Weakness: Hard to interpret why it made a decision.
🎮 Behaviorism
Believes intelligence is about behavior and interaction with the environment. Reward good behavior, penalize bad. This is the basis of Reinforcement Learning (e.g., AlphaGo).
Strength: Can discover superhuman strategies. Weakness: Needs enormous trial-and-error experience.
⚠ Exam Alert
Know all three schools by name and their core idea: Symbolism = rules & logic, Connectionism = neural networks, Behaviorism = reward-based learning.

5. Three Elements That Power AI Development

Why did AI suddenly take off around 2012? Three things came together at once:

📦
Data
The internet, smartphones, and sensors generated billions of labelled examples for AI to learn from.
⚡
Computing Power
GPUs (and later AI chips like Huawei's Ascend) made training large neural networks affordable.
🧮
Algorithms
Breakthroughs in backpropagation, CNNs, attention, and transformers gave AI the right techniques.
⚠ Exam Alert
The three elements are: Data, Computing Power, Algorithms. Memorize this triad — it appears frequently.

6. Weak AI vs. Strong AI

🔵 Weak AI (Narrow AI)
Can do one specific thing very well — but only that thing. Example: a chess-playing AI can't write poetry. A face-recognition system can't drive a car. Everything that exists today is Weak AI.
🔴 Strong AI (Artificial General Intelligence — AGI)
Can do anything a human can do — reason, plan, understand, learn across domains. This does not exist yet. It is the long-term goal of the field.
Quick check
Is ChatGPT Weak AI or Strong AI? — It's Weak AI. It's extraordinarily capable at language tasks, but it can't drive a car, see the physical world, or act in it like a general-purpose agent.
