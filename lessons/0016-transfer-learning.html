Transfer Learning & Fine-tuning

How to build powerful models with little data by borrowing knowledge from models already trained on millions of examples.

1. The Problem Transfer Learning Solves

Training a deep network from scratch requires:

  • Millions of labelled examples (ImageNet has 1.2 million images)
  • Weeks of compute time on many GPUs or NPUs
  • Expert knowledge to design and tune the architecture

Most real projects have none of these. A hospital might have 500 labelled X-rays. A startup might need a product photo classifier with only 200 images per category. Training from scratch on this data would massively overfit — the model would memorise those few examples and fail on anything new.

Analogy
You already know how to drive a car. Learning to drive a truck doesn't mean forgetting everything and starting from scratch — you transfer your knowledge of steering, braking, and road rules, then learn only what's different (a larger vehicle, different mirrors, air brakes). Transfer learning does the same: reuse knowledge from a large task, adapt only what needs to change.

2. Why Learned Features Transfer

In Lesson 11 you learned that a CNN's layers learn a hierarchy of features: early layers detect edges and colours, middle layers detect textures and shapes, deep layers detect high-level object parts. These low- and mid-level features are general — an edge detector useful for dogs is equally useful for cats, cars, or medical images.

3. Two Strategies: Feature Extraction vs Fine-tuning

Strategy 1 — Feature Extraction (Freeze everything)
Keep all pre-trained weights unchanged. Remove the original classifier head and attach a new one. Only the new head is trained.

When to use: Your dataset is small (<1,000 examples per class) or your domain is very different from the pre-training domain (e.g. ImageNet → medical scans).
Risk if you don't freeze: With little data, updating all weights causes overfitting — the model memorises your few examples.
Strategy 2 — Fine-tuning (Unfreeze some or all layers)
After training the new head, unfreeze the last few layers (or all layers) and continue training at a very low learning rate (10–100× smaller than usual). This gently nudges the pre-trained weights toward your task.

When to use: You have enough data to avoid overfitting, and your task domain is similar to the pre-training domain.
Why low LR: A high learning rate would destroy the general features already learned — you only want to fine-tune, not re-learn from scratch.

Choose your strategy based on how much data you have and how similar your task is to the source domain.

⚠ Exam Alert
Two transfer learning strategies: Feature Extraction — freeze all pre-trained layers, train new head only (for small datasets). Fine-tuning — unfreeze some/all layers and train at a very low learning rate (for larger datasets or when you need maximum performance). Fine-tuning at a high LR destroys the pre-trained features.

4. Common Pre-trained Models

Model Domain Architecture Transfer use
ResNet-50 Images 50-layer CNN with residual connections Most image classification and detection tasks
VGG-16 Images 16-layer CNN, simple and uniform Style transfer, feature visualisation
BERT Text (English) Transformer encoder, 110M params Classification, NER, question answering
PanGu-α Text (Chinese) GPT-style decoder, 200B params Huawei industry fine-tuning (government, mining…)
ViT Images Transformer applied to image patches Large-scale image tasks, replacing CNNs
Residual connections — why ResNet transfers so well
ResNet introduced skip connections: each block adds its input directly to its output. This prevents vanishing gradients in very deep networks and means each layer only needs to learn a residual correction rather than a full transformation. It's the same Add & Norm idea you saw in the Transformer (Lesson 13) — both borrowed from the same insight.

5. Transfer Learning in MindSpore

Here is the complete pattern for feature extraction using a pre-trained ResNet-50 in MindSpore:

Step 1 — Load a pre-trained model and replace the head
import mindspore.nn as nn
from mindvision.classification.models import resnet50

# Load ResNet-50 with ImageNet weights
backbone = resnet50(pretrained=True)

# Replace the final FC layer (1000 classes → 5 flower classes)
backbone.classifier = nn.Dense(2048, 5)
Step 2 — Freeze the backbone, train only the new head
# Freeze all backbone parameters
for param in backbone.get_parameters():
    param.requires_grad = False

# Unfreeze only the new classifier head
for param in backbone.classifier.get_parameters():
    param.requires_grad = True

# Now only the head's params will be updated by the optimiser
optimizer = nn.Adam(
    params=filter(lambda p: p.requires_grad,
                  backbone.trainable_params()),
    learning_rate=0.001
)
Step 3 (optional) — Fine-tune: unfreeze all layers at a low LR
# After training the head for a few epochs, unfreeze everything
for param in backbone.get_parameters():
    param.requires_grad = True

# Use a much lower learning rate to avoid destroying pre-trained features
optimizer_finetune = nn.Adam(
    params=backbone.trainable_params(),
    learning_rate=1e-5    # 100× smaller than the head-training LR
)
⚠ Exam Alert
In MindSpore, you control which layers train by setting param.requires_grad = False (freeze) or True (unfreeze). The optimiser only updates parameters where requires_grad=True. Fine-tuning always uses a much lower learning rate than initial training to avoid overwriting pre-learned features.

6. Why Transfer Learning Dominates Real Projects

Much less data needed
A model fine-tuned from ResNet-50 can achieve excellent accuracy with only a few hundred images per class. Training the same architecture from scratch might need 100,000+.
Much faster training
Training only a small head takes minutes instead of days. Even full fine-tuning converges much faster because the weights start in a good region.
Better generalisation
Pre-trained weights encode robust, general representations built from vastly more data than you'll ever have. Your fine-tuned model inherits this robustness.
Enables domain-specific AI
This is how Huawei's PanGu 3-tier hierarchy works (Lesson 03): the base LLM is pre-trained on vast general data → industry model is fine-tuned on domain data → scenario model is fine-tuned further on task-specific data.
