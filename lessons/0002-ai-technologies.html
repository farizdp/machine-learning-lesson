AI Technologies: NLP, Computer Vision & Foundation Models

A map of what AI can actually do — the major technical fields you need to know for the exam.

In the last lesson, you learned that AI is a broad field. But what does it actually do? In practice, AI research is divided into a handful of major technical directions. You need to know these — both their names and what they solve.

The Big Picture
Think of AI as a toolbox. The toolbox has several compartments: NLP (for language), Computer Vision (for images/video), Speech Processing (for sound), Decision-making Systems, and Big Data Analysis. Today we focus on the two most important for the exam: NLP and CV.

1. Natural Language Processing (NLP)

NLP is the branch of AI that deals with human language — text and speech. "Natural language" just means the language humans naturally speak and write (as opposed to programming languages).

NLP breaks down into four main task types:

📂 Text Classification
Assign a label to a piece of text.
Examples: Is this email spam or not? Is this review positive or negative? What topic is this news article about?
🏷 Sequence Labelling
Tag each word in a sentence with a label.
Example: In "Apple was founded by Steve Jobs in California" — tag "Apple" as ORG, "Steve Jobs" as PERSON, "California" as LOCATION. This is called Named Entity Recognition (NER).
✍️ Text Generation
Produce new text given some input.
Examples: Machine translation (English → Chinese), summarization, question answering, chatbots. ChatGPT is a text generation system.
🕸 Knowledge Graph
Build a structured map of facts and their relationships.
Example: "Paris" → is capital of → "France" → is in → "Europe". Search engines use these to answer factual questions directly.
⚠ Exam Alert
Know all four NLP tasks: Text Classification, Sequence Labelling, Text Generation, Knowledge Graph. Know one real-world example for each.

2. Computer Vision (CV)

Computer Vision is the branch of AI that deals with images and video — giving machines the ability to "see" and interpret visual information.

🎯 Object Detection
Find and locate objects in an image, drawing a box around each one.
Example: A self-driving car detecting pedestrians, traffic lights, and other vehicles in real time.
✂️ Image Segmentation
Go beyond bounding boxes — colour every pixel according to what it belongs to.
Example: In a medical scan, colour the tumour pixels red and healthy tissue blue so doctors can measure it precisely.
🎥 Object Tracking
Follow a specific object across multiple frames of a video.
Example: Tracking a footballer's movement across an entire match for performance analysis.
🔤 OCR (Optical Character Recognition)
Read text from images.
Example: Scanning a handwritten form and converting it to editable text; reading license plates from a camera.
🎨 Image Generation
Create new images from scratch, or from a text description.
Example: Stable Diffusion, DALL-E — type "a cat in a spacesuit" and get a realistic image.
⚠ Exam Alert
Know all five CV tasks: Object Detection, Image Segmentation, Object Tracking, OCR, Image Generation. The distinction between Object Detection (boxes around objects) and Image Segmentation (pixel-level labelling) is commonly tested.

3. Foundation Models — A Revolution in AI

Until around 2020, most AI systems were trained for one task. An image classifier only classified images. A translation model only translated. Then something changed.

Foundation Models are massive AI models trained on enormous amounts of diverse data, which can then be adapted to many different tasks. They are the basis of ChatGPT, DALL-E, and Huawei's PanGu.

Analogy
Think of a Foundation Model like a university graduate with a broad education. They don't know everything about surgery, law, or engineering — but they have a strong general foundation that lets them be trained quickly for any of those specializations. Before Foundation Models, every AI was like someone who only ever studied one subject and couldn't switch.

Where Do Foundation Models Come From? The Transformer

Foundation Models are built on an architecture called the Transformer (introduced by Google in 2017). The Transformer introduced the attention mechanism — a way for the model to figure out which parts of the input are most relevant to focus on. We'll cover this in detail in Chapter 03.

There are three flavours of Transformer architecture:

Type What it does best Example models
Encoder-Only Understanding text (classification, NER, search) BERT, RoBERTa
Decoder-Only Generating text (chat, code, completion) GPT-4, DeepSeek, LLaMA
Encoder + Decoder Transforming text (translation, summarization) T5, BART, original Transformer
⚠ Exam Alert
Know the three Transformer types and their use cases. Encoder-only = understanding. Decoder-only = generation. Both = transformation. BERT is Encoder-only; GPT is Decoder-only.

Emergence — The Surprise Property

Something unexpected happens when you make Foundation Models very large: they develop abilities that nobody explicitly trained them for. This is called emergence.

A small language model might only finish sentences. A large one suddenly can do arithmetic, write code, translate languages, and reason about abstract problems — without being specifically trained on those tasks. These abilities "emerge" from scale.

Key idea
Emergence means: the whole is more than the sum of its parts. Just adding more data and parameters produces qualitatively new capabilities, not just "more of the same."

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) — Teaching AI to Think Step by Step

By default, a language model generates an answer in one shot. But if you prompt it to show its reasoning — "Think step by step" — it dramatically improves on complex tasks. This technique is called Chain-of-Thought (CoT).

Analogy
If someone asks you "What is 17 × 23?", you probably can't do it instantly. But if you write it out: 17 × 20 = 340, 17 × 3 = 51, total = 391 — you get there. CoT is prompting the AI to do the same.
